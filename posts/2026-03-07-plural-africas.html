Plural Africa: creolization, polyculturalism, and the emergence of new identities

by Nathan McNee · 7 Mar 2026 · ~2 min read

Public historians often make the mistake of treating Africa like a single point of origin. This article shows how Lowcountry records hint at multiple African origins, and explains why that matters.

Why “plural Africa” matters

When we confidently claim that Africans brought their culture to the Lowcountry, it can sound respectful, but it quietly erases difference. Africa becomes a single origin, and the enslaved become a single people. This flattened story erases the real story of how enslaved people built community across real divisions of language and culture, making it easier to label African knowledge as some vague “influence” rather than a specific set of skills and memories.

Instead of imagining the silhouette of a continent, image a coastline with many ports and many inland worlds behind them. The trans-Atlantic slave trade drew captives from a wide swath of West and West-Central Africa, peoples brought together by a shared experience but otherwise separated by origin. Together, they forged a set of new cultures, different than any they had left behind, but recognizably related. Scholars call this process “creolization.”

Creolization and polyculturalism

Creolization is what happens when two or more very different cultures collide. It’s a process, and contact is not enough by itself; it takes forced co-existence to create a new, singular, and shared identity. Imperialism is the usual cause, and the usual result is a “creole” language and culture, formed through the selective exchange of several languages and cultures. This was especially prevalent in Lousiana, the West Indies, and the Lowcountry.

Michel-Rolph Trouillot, however, calls creolization “a miracle begging for analysis.”1 In other words, it is the result of a process we don’t quite understand. Ras Michael Brown picks up on this, observing that the word “creolization” is often used to take a position on the debate between continuity and creativity. Each side of this debate is making an implicitly political choice about how we ought to weigh two different emphases: the agency of the enslaved, and the cruelty of the enslaver. Brown argues, however, that continuity and creativity are not opposites, but “interdependent processes” resulting from the same agency, resolving both the political and the academic dilemma.2

According to Brown, the Lowcountry began as a “polycultural” society — in other words, a place where many different cultures run in parallel, talking without merging. Over time, however, as the slave trade died down, most new Lowcountry slaves were born in the Americas, and so a unified creole culture slowly emerged. But this creole is the reflection of what he calls a “culture of volition.” In other words, it was the result of the choices people made about how they would make sense of the world around them. Agency can be found in both the changes from and the continuities with their past.

Gullah, a Lowcountry creole, is a good example of this process. It preserved African grammar and vocabulary while still being shaped by the English language. Having collectively built it, Gullah itself is a reflection of the agency of the enslaved — a set of choices about what matters and what is worth remembering.

Takeaway

The point is not to complicate the picture beyond comprehension, but to resist a simple narrative of singular origin. The Lowcountry was an African–Atlantic place, built through forced migration from multiple regions, then held together by the creative work of the enslaved as they made a cultural world of their own faced with the violence of a system built to subjugate. Agency is everywhere, if we are willing to look.
