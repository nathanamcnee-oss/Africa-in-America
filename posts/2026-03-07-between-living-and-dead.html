Between the living and the dead: the making of a creole landscape

by Nathan McNee · 7 Mar 2026 · ~5 min read

The simbi, nature spirits of the Kongo, enabled enslaved Africans to turn a strange land into a home, rebuilding community though cut off from their ancestors. This article explores the creolization of place, revealing how Africa persisted not only in language and practice but in the meaning of the landscape itself.

The gods of Africa

For the Gullah, the simbi were an integral part of the Lowcountry landscape.1 They were mediators, a point of connection between the physical land of the living and the spiritual land of the dead. But they were also a connection between the past and the future, between one’s ancestors and one’s children, between Africa and America. They were at the center of the “miracle” of creolization.2 And yet, somehow, they are almost always forgotten when we tell the story of slavery and freedom in the Lowcountry.

Scholars often claim that “the gods of Africa died” in what became the United States.3 But where the Lowcountry is concerned, this thesis simply does not hold. The simbi were creole gods, carried over and reshaped to fit the needs of the emerging Gullah people. On one level, they were a means of mapping social life onto the environment — where it was safe to go, what counted as trespass, how to interact with the landscape.4 But on another level, the simbi were a response to the experience of exile, a way to establish a relationship with a place their ancestors did not know. Ultimately — in Ras Michael Brown’s powerful words — it was through the simbi that “the living declared that they belonged to the land and that the land belonged to them.”5

The sacred cross of the Kongo

There is no single “Kongo cosmology.” Though diversity can be overstated, a variety of concepts persisted all the way across the Atlantic. But this variety is mere variation on a shared theme. The Bakongo tended to imagine the world as two mountains, perfectly mirrored, arising in opposite directions out of a shared body of water. This body of water is the Kalûnga (literally “threshold between worlds”). The first mountain is the land of the living (“nseke”), which we inhabit, and the other side is the land of the dead (“mpèmba”), where we pass upon our death.6

When the sun sets on our world, it rises in the land of the dead, returning to us with sunrise. Including midday on both mountains, the movement of the sun entails four stages. And just like the sun, the seasons, and the elements, we also live in four stages. We are born in the land of the living, attain maturity at midday, die at sunset, and then rise with the sun in the land of the dead, before we are once again drawn by conception into rebirth. In terms of the seasons, this is summer, fall, winter, and spring. In terms of the elements, it is fire, earth, water, and air. This forms the Kongo “cosmogram,” a sacred symbol — a cross.7

The simbi are nature spirits who inhabit the water. Since the Kalûnga, the water, is the “threshold between worlds,” so are they. Not only do they mediate between nature and its inhabitants, they also mediate between these two worlds. Through them, the living can reach the dead. Through them, the dead can reach the living.8

Where your ancestors do not live

The simbi are still integral to Bakongo religion, but five centuries ago — as the Altantic world first emerged — they played a unique and indispensable role in preserving the Bakongo people. According to a Kongo proverb, “Where your ancestors do not live, you cannot build your house.”9 As the rapidly changing Atlantic world displaced African peoples, this belief bred a conundrum. If you find yourself in a land where your ancestors never lived, how can you make it your home?

Although this problem is most obviously relevant where the enslaved are concerned, the emerging Atlantic world displaced Bakongo peoples within Africa before they were ever brought beyond it. By the time captives arrived in the Lowcountry, their cultures had already been dealing with this problem for over a century. And for many, the simbi were the solution.10

Once someone had passed into the land of the dead (mpèmba), many believed that the soul could choose to return not past the Kalûnga (water threshold), but to it, as a simbi. In doing so, they became the ancestors of all people. This allowed the simbi to stand in for a community’s past, making homemaking possible beyond the realm of home.11

But the simbi were also nature spirits, features and representatives of the landscape. They offered a means to make order out of the chaos of the natural world. The simbi governed the rains, the soil, the plants, and the animals so that these strangers in a strange land could speak to the land and make it their own. And in some sense, this worked: the enslaved developed a mastery of the landscape that the Whites of the region never did.12

As Africans of different cultures were brought, captive, to the Lowcountry, the simbi played a crucial role in the construction of a cultural medium that could form the foundation for a new community. All arrivals had been stripped from their ancestors, all were in an unknown land, and all faced the monumental task of making their world comprehensible. The polycultural complexities of African belief evolved, through intercultural exchange, into a creolized culture that formed the foundation for a new, shared Gullah identity.13

Afterlives of the simbi

The simbi remained an integral part of Gullah practice and belief for at least two centuries. Not only Kikongo simbi-related words survive in the Gullah language, the imagined landscape of the Lowcountry long continued to be shaped by these nature spirits. Even once Christianity arrived, the simbi played key roles in a creolized Christianity. Entering the 20th century, these spirits even influenced the concept of mermaids.14

In the early decades of the nineteenth century, a man named Dr. Henry Ravenel decided — as part of a plantation revitalization project — to build a wall enclosing a limestone spring. The Gullah inhabitants of the Lowcountry believed that these limestone springs were the residence of the simbi. Believing that this would offend the nature spirit, an elder of Africa and Native descent confronted Ravenel, proclaiming that the spring would disappear if he built the wall. Ravenel did so anyway.15

A century later, a 1930s Gullah Christian minister claimed that baptism occurred in the outgoing tide so “the tide can carry the sins on out to the deep of the sea,” where the incoming tides would wash them “right back up.” Brown argues that this, alongside other features of Gullah Christianity (“seeking,” white babies, white bundles), is a relic of Bakongo religion. The water rite represents “passing through the [Kalûnga] once again,” leaving the “land of the dead.”16

Brown goes on to argue that the simbi was not displaced by mermaids, sirène, and mami wata, but influenced them, creating some of the cultural forms we see today. The name can change and the idea yet persist.17

Conclusion

The simbi, preserved but reshaped in Gullah culture, helped make it possible for West Africans — displaced by the slave trade — to make a home out of a strange land and to make the landscape of the Lowcountry both accessible and malleable. These simbi were not a relic of the past, a chance survival of a brutal trade; they were a deliberate choice made by Africans who, through their agency, created a culture of their own in America. It would do us well to remember that agency as we study the past and create our own future.18
